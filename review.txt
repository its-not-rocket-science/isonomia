Review #1

Governance Phase Space, Lock-In, and Schismogenesis: A Computational Framework for Long-Run Freedom Dynamics
Overview
This is an interesting and timely piece insofar as both subjective impressions and quantitative measures such as Freedom House scores suggest that the high legibility furnished by digital systems, through which a growing fraction of social intercourse takes place, is putting societies, including those traditionally understood as part of the ìfree worldî, under surveillance and control mechanisms of growing scope and specificity, while simultaneously eroding democratic responsiveness and accountability. The paperís attempt to integrate comparative historical analysis, governance measurement, governance succession dynamics, network analysis, and agent-based modelling is ambitious and potentially valuable. 
There are, however, gaps in the exposition (e.g. defining terms, explaining where some coded values come from). There is also a seemingly non-Batesonian understanding of schismogenesis, whereby schismogenesis occurs at long range, as an outcome of local clustering arising from homophilic/heterophobic processes, rather than at short range as an outcome of interaction itself.
Recommendation
I recommend publication subject to the following observations and concerns, which should strengthen the paper and make its contribution easier to evaluate.
1. Definitions and exposition in the Introduction
Rather than relying on definitions introduced in the abstract, it would be desirable if key variables could be defined in the main text as they are introduced. For example, in Section 1.1, the reader needs to remember the abstract in order to understand the phrase ìL-conditional stationary distributionsî. It might be better to say something like ìa continuous-time Markov chain (CTMC) succession model producing stationary distributions conditional on surplus legibility (L).î 
It would also be desirable to explain the phrase ìsurplus legibilityî for those who are unfamiliar with James C. Scottís work and may think it just means something like literacy or record-keeping.
2. Measurement framework and validation
Table 1 does not clearly specify whether the external variables are being correlated with SAP, EDR, or individual component variables such as E, D, R, S, A, or P. The abstract clarifies some, such as the WJP Rule of Law Index correlation being with E, but not all. Each table row should identify the isonomia-side variable to which the external variable is being compared. 
The construction of the composite metrics SAP and EDR should also be made explicit. If, say, SAP is just the arithmetic mean of S, A, and P, this should be stated.
The resilience threshold ? ? 0.45 requires clearer derivation. The text states that this threshold is the EDR level below which ìTurchin-Tainter collapse dynamics become significantly more probable,î but Figure 1 appears to show collapse modes rather than collapse probabilities, so it is not clear how the threshold is drawn. Are certain collapse modes deemed to represent ìTurchin-Tainter collapse dynamicsî, which, incidentally, are not defined or referenced? What does ìsignificantlyî mean in this context? It would be desirable to discuss uncertainty or robustness checks, if any.
3. The lock-in sequence and L values
The paragraph beginning ìFour case studies trace this sequenceî reports precise L values for Natufian communities, Ubaid, Early Uruk, Qin Legalism, the Inca Empire, and Zomia Highland Communities. However, the source and coding procedure for these values are unclear. Scott 2009 may support the conceptual claim that Zomia maintained low legibility through state-evading practices, but it does not appear to provide a general quantitative scale of surplus legibility for the other historical cases. It would be desirable to indicate where these values come from.
4. Succession mechanism dynamics and CTMC
While section 4.1 refers to ì117 hand-coded governance systemsî and a working-set criterion of ìcoding confidence ? 2,î the confidence scale is not defined. If this uses a known procedure, it would be desirable to see it referenced. Otherwise, it would be desirable to see things like the range of the scale, the criteria for each level, who assigned confidence scores, whether multiple coders were used, how disagreements were resolved, and whether intercoder reliability was assessed. This matters because the reported cross-tabulations, Mann-Whitney comparison, and odds ratios depend on the resulting working set.
The formula used for the OR reported in Figure 2 and Section 4.2 should ideally be stated so that readers can tell exactly what contrast the odds ratios quantify.
5. Citation network and schismogenesis
The term ìcitation networkî is insufficiently defined. Similarly, the terms comparator, parallel, and contrast as relationship types are not defined, albeit that the implications of comparator and contrast can be inferred from the context. What constitutes a citation and how were relationships classified into comparator, parallel, and contrast? Do edges represent historical contact, textual or source citation, modern scholarly comparison, or dataset-internal coding?
The interpretation of the ìdegree-4 sign reversalî as evidence for schismogenesis is not convincing. From Figure 3 and the text, ìdegreeî here appears to mean shortest-path network distance, not node degree (i.e. it has the same meaning as in ësix degrees of separationí). If schismogenesis is a mechanism of differentiation through contact, one would expect the strongest negative association among directly linked contrast pairs. Instead, EDR correlation is positive at distances 1ñ3 and negative only at distance 4, meaning that there is contagion at short range and differentiation at long range, the opposite of differentiation through contact. The paper seems to be arguing for two effectsóshort range clustering and then medium to long range schismogenesis, or schismogenesis between the clusters, which may be the case but is not really in the spirit of Batesonís model. It makes schismogenesis look more like a by-product of the limited range of the contagion effect rather than a positive drive to differentiate. In any case, it needs to be explained why it is the differentiation of elements specifically four degrees apart that proves schismogenesis rather than say three or five.
The lack of influence of geographic proximity on the amount of difference between societies, as shown in the centre panel of Figure 3, also seems to speak against the idea of schismogenesis, unless it is to be argued that the intensity of interaction between societies is unrelated to their geographic proximity. The paper states that ìschismogenesis is ideational not spatialî. This just seems to be saying that societies that are already ideationally different become even more different, which is not the schismogenesis argument that interaction creates differentiation. If this is a misunderstanding of the paperís argument, then it would be desirable for the paper to explain itself more clearly so that this misunderstanding cannot arise.
6. Agent-based model
The ABM is presented as a model of schismogenesis, but its operational structure is closer to a one-dimensional attraction-repulsion model. Agents close in EDR are treated as comparators and converge; agents distant in EDR are treated as contrasts and diverge. Again, this is not structurally equivalent to Batesonian schismogenesis, where differentiation arises through contact or interaction measured independently of the trait on which divergence occurs. A stronger model would require an independent dimension of awareness, salience, proximity, rivalry, or contact, and a separate outcome dimension such as EDR.
The model risks building the schismogenesis mechanism into its update rule rather than demonstrating it. Because edge type is defined by pre-existing EDR distance, the finding that contrast edges produce greater EDR divergence is difficult to interpret as evidence of interaction coming first and divergence emerging later, i.e. of a historical direction to the process. The model may show that threshold-based attraction and repulsion can generate bipolarity, but it does not by itself show that contact between governance systems caused deliberate institutional differentiation.
In the parameter sweep leading to the construction of the ìloss landscapeî in Figure 5, the reported optimum occurs at ? = 0.20, i.e. the lower boundary of the swept range. This suggests it might be worth searching beyond the boundary, unless there is some theoretical reason for not doing so.
The phrase ìcross-theta contrast fraction too highî is difficult to assess because the statistic is not defined or quantified. Assuming it refers to the proportion of contrast edges connecting systems on opposite sides of the EDR resilience threshold ? ? 0.45, it would be desirable to report the quantitative calculation underlying the claim rather than just mentioning it in passing.
7. Discussion and contemporary implications
Section 6.2 refers to an ìABM calibration ceiling,î presumably referring to residual mismatch between the best-calibrated ABM and the empirical citation network. While the paper suggests this ceiling supports the Graeber-Wengrow thesis, sceptical readers might wonder if it is simply an indication of model misspecification or uncertainty in edge coding, and it would be desirable to address this more strongly.
The statement in Section 6.3 that schismogenesis is ìconsistent with contemporary evidence of democratic backsliding in systems with high exposure to authoritarian governance modelsî is unclear. One might expect ìdemocratic backslidingî to denote movement toward less democratic or more authoritarian governance, which would seem to imply high exposure producing convergence with authoritarian models rather than intensified divergence from them as schismogenesis would predict. Here we return to the same issue mentioned in the context of the empirical network and the ABM, namely a mismatch between the Batesonian vision of schismogenesis as divergence resulting from high interaction intensity and the paperís apparent vision of schismogenesis as somehow involving convergence through high interaction intensity with subsequent divergence between the resulting local clusters.
It is possible that the paper is intending a more elaborate mechanism (e.g. coercive influence) that paradoxically allows schismogenesis to produce convergence, or it is possible that ìdemocratic backslidingî means a move towards not away from democracy, but if so, this should be made clearer.
8. Limitations and conclusion
The proposed future replacement of ìdistance-based edge formationî with ìhistorically structured awareness setsî usefully identifies a limitation of the present ABM. In context, ìdistance-basedî appears to mean distance in EDR space, not geographical distance. The manuscript should explicitly distinguish EDR distance, network distance, and geographic distance here and in other areas where they might be confused. More importantly, the proposed improvement underlines that the current model lacks the independent historical-contact layer required to distinguish schismogenesis through contact from threshold-based divergence among already dissimilar systems.


Review #2

Review

This paper attempts to 'operationalise' and quantitatively test the claims of Graeber and Wengrow's (G&W) 'asymmetric freedom loss' as 'legibility' (i.e., ability of the state to monitor production and taxes), among other measures, increases. †The freedoms discussed are the freedom to 'exit' the society, the freedom to 'disobey' societal injuctions, and freedom to experiment with alternative political arrangements.† The paper suggests that as these freedoms were lost that societal resilience decreased and the possibility of collapse increased. †'Resilience' and 'collapse' are not defined or discussed very well: What would 'collapse' mean in a society where you can walk away or try a new arrangement? †Is that high resilience? †Is it only collapse when some previous, long-lived, arrangement is abandoned?


But whatever. No one disagrees that some versions of these freedoms were reduced or disappeared as states grew in scale and administrative competence, though this paper, like G&W itself, does not discuss what was gained by societies in exchange for these losses (better public goods, larger populations and markets and hence better goods and services, better defense, etc.) and whether people would rather remain rather than being unable to leave such a society, etc.† As usual with discussions framed using Graeber and Wengrow's writings, there is a strong rhetorical spin where charged words do a lot of the lifting (freedoms to dissent and resilience lost, chance of collapse increased, lock-in occurring, etc.). †


The paper, while suffering from these issues, nevertheless tries to make a quantitative contribution by operationalizing and measuring these terms and demonstrate these patterns. And it is here that the paper falls immediately short.


It is clear, as various tools confirm, that substantial amounts of the paper, the code and the even the data has been created with AI assistance (likely claude) yet this is nowhere acknowledged, as it should. †While not disqualifying by itself it is worrisome that it was not disclosed. In addition, the author (easily identified because his ORCID was not removed) has no prior publication record or affiliation in these areas or other adjacent fields that suggest particular competence at doing these sorts of studies or having access to peer groups that could help vet approaches and results; there are no co-authors, for example. †Again, not disqualifying but this heightens our concern and scrutiny, which is sadly rewarded by looking at the data the paper is based upon.


Most of the statistics of paper is based on a dataset (governance_extended.csv according to the code or, given their data columns required, 99083990-bc6b-4c46-8573-6ed127745645.csv in the zip) that the author and the AI composed. †It contains 390 entries of 'systems' (societies) that have several characteristics coded from 0-1 that are combined to make the main dimensions of their 'phase space' ('SAP' and 'ERD'). †Apparently, the author created 125 of these entries by hand (?) and had the AI provide the other 264 (67%); these can be identified trivially by the annotation 'requires human review' in the 'notes' column. †There is no discussion of any attempt to provide that human review; no procedure, no named humans with plausible expertise. The author provides few criteria about why these specific polities were included and others not included. Of course the G&W examples are included but those are few (14 according to the 'gw_discussed' column).


While the .md file presents the numeric coding scheme for the main characteristics, there is no discussion about how the values of the key entries (sovereignty_index, admin_index, competitive_politics_index, exit_freedom, disobedience_freedom, arrangement_freedom, surplus_legibility, info_infrastructure, etc.) were encoded from whatever sources were used. †The specific scholarly sources or databases are not listed for each entry or included in the bibliography.


However encoded, the paper then presents a cross-validation table (Table 1) of (a subset of?) †the hand-coded entries with other known databases but does not indicate what (numeric? also converted?) bits of data in those databases were used to make these comparisons or why those were appropriate variables for the comparisons. †There is no supplemental information file that would present and discuss this critical methodology in any detail as is expected in a scholarly paper. †And if such a cross-correlation can be accomplished why can't the other AI entries be treated the same way and reduce our concern that those entries were not 'hallucinated'. †Having some experience with this on other historical data projects, this is not a trivial concern.


Until that basic vetting and description is provided, we can have no trust in the data or any of the subsequent observations or conclusions of this paper, however obvious and in alignment with expectation or prior results. †The additional work on various figures and statistics (all now easily done with AI assistance) are all meaningless without this basic work or justification for why these specific tests were applied. †This paper, in its present form, is unacceptable as a scholarly contribution to the journal.
