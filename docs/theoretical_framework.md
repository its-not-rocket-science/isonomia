# Theoretical Framework
## isonomia — Governance Emergence, Resilience, and Dissolution

---

## 1. The Problem: Two Literatures, One Loop

The study of governance in deep history is split between two bodies of work that rarely engage with each other.

**Collapse models** — principally Tainter (1988), Turchin (2003, 2016), and the HANDY framework (Motesharrei et al. 2014) — ask: given that complex hierarchical societies exist, why do they sometimes fail? The explanatory variables are resource stress, elite overextraction, and declining marginal returns on administrative complexity. Collapse is the anomaly requiring a cause.

**Emergence models** — principally Mann (1986), Fukuyama (2011), and most recently Graeber & Wengrow (2021) — ask: given that humans spent most of their history in flexible, relatively egalitarian arrangements, why do rigid hierarchical societies emerge and persist? Here hierarchy is the anomaly requiring a cause.

These frameworks address opposite ends of the same causal loop. What is missing is a unified model that handles both the emergence of hierarchy *and* its dissolution, without treating either as the natural baseline. This paper proposes such a model.

---

## 2. The Graeber-Wengrow Challenge

Graeber & Wengrow's *The Dawn of Everything* (2021) mounts the most systematic recent challenge to stadial models of social evolution. Four claims are directly relevant to model construction:

**Seasonality and deliberate oscillation.** Many societies alternated between governance modes — hierarchical during winter aggregation and ceremonial periods, egalitarian during summer dispersal. This is not instability; it is designed flexibility. A model must accommodate societies as moving through a phase space, not occupying fixed positions on a typological ladder.

**Three elementary forms of domination.** G&W identify sovereignty (charismatic violence, the capacity to kill), administration (bureaucratic record-keeping, surveillance), and competitive/heroic politics (prestige, performance, charisma) as the three independent sources from which hierarchical arrangements are built. Crucially, these can be combined in different proportions — most existing typologies conflate them.

**Three freedoms as political self-consciousness.** Against the three forms of domination, G&W identify three freedoms that pre-state societies understood and often explicitly defended: the freedom to move away (exit), the freedom to disobey (refuse authority), and the freedom to create new social arrangements. These are not romanticised primitives — they are documented political values whose deliberate suppression is the precondition for state formation.

**Getting stuck.** G&W argue that large-scale, permanently hierarchical societies emerged not through evolutionary inevitability but through specific historical accidents — the combination of cereal agriculture (which bound populations to land), grain-based surplus legibility (which enabled taxation), and literate administration (which enabled persistent obligation-tracking). Societies with all three, simultaneously, tended to lose the three freedoms and became unable to self-correct.

## 3. The SAP Phase Space

Following G&W's three elementary forms, each governance system is positioned in a continuous three-dimensional space:

**S — Sovereignty index (0–1)**: Concentration of coercive power in identifiable authority. Anchors: San Xaro Networks (0.05) → Egyptian Old Kingdom (0.90).

**A — Administration index (0–1)**: Extent and sophistication of record-keeping and bureaucratic apparatus. Anchors: Highland New Guinea Big Man System (0.05) → Confucian Bureaucracy (0.95).

**P — Competitive politics index (0–1)**: Degree to which politics operates through prestige competition, performance, and heroic display. Anchors: Egyptian Old Kingdom (0.20) → Balinese Negara (0.95).

These three dimensions are not independent. High-A systems tend to channel P into administrative competition (examinations, cursus honorum) and suppress unmediated S. High-S systems tend to eliminate P as a threat to sovereign authority. The SAP ternary is a phase space with regions of relative stability and transition zones between them.

Most historically durable large states occupy a band where all three are moderately to highly elevated simultaneously — the "full lock-in" zone. What G&W demonstrate is that this combination is historically contingent, not inevitable: many large-scale, complex societies maintained low S+A while sustaining high P (Northwest Coast Potlatch cultures) or low S+P while sustaining high A (Inca Empire) for centuries.

## 4. The EDR Resilience Composite

The three Graeber-Wengrow freedoms are operationalised as:

**E — Exit freedom (0–1)**: Can members leave the governance system without catastrophic cost? Anchors: Tokugawa Shogunate (0.05, sankin-kōtai hostage system) → Zomia highland communities (0.95, designed mobility).

**D — Disobedience freedom (0–1)**: Can authority be refused through legitimate or customary channels? Anchors: Tokugawa Shogunate (0.05) → Gadaa System (0.85, age-set rotation structurally encodes D).

**R — Arrangement freedom (0–1)**: Can members create new social institutions or reorganise existing ones? Anchors: Tokugawa Shogunate (0.05) → Maroon Communities (0.85, explicit reconstruction of governance from scratch).

The **EDR composite** is defined as (E + D + R) / 3.

### The Central Hypothesis

*Societies where the EDR composite exceeds a threshold θ retain self-correcting capacity; those below θ enter a fragile regime where Turchin/Tainter collapse dynamics become operative.*

The mechanism is as follows. High EDR provides error-correction: bad leadership is abandoned (E), bad decisions are refused (D), failing institutions are replaced (R). When EDR drops below θ — through rising surplus legibility (L) enabling taxation, rising information infrastructure (I) enabling persistent obligation-tracking, and the consequent rise in A and suppression of E and D — this error-correction mechanism is dismantled. The system can no longer self-correct through internal reorganisation and becomes dependent on external perturbation (invasion, climate shock, elite civil war) to reset.

Preliminary analysis of the 398-society dataset (hand-coded n=73, auto-coded n=325) finds EDR-SAP correlation r = –0.763 (hand-coded subset: r = –0.844), consistent with the hypothesis that SAP elevation systematically suppresses the three freedoms.

### The Lock-In Sequence

The specific mechanism by which EDR is suppressed follows a predictable sequence:

1. Rising **L** (surplus legibility) — cereal agriculture, fixed territory — enables extraction
2. Rising **L** drives rising **A** (administration) — record-keeping apparatus needed to manage extraction
3. Rising **A** suppresses **E** — administrative systems require stable populations to extract from; exit becomes illegal or economically catastrophic
4. With **E** suppressed, **D** weakens — the threat of exit was the primary leverage for refusing authority
5. With **E** and **D** suppressed, **R** is eliminated — social arrangements are redefined as natural or sacred rather than chosen
6. EDR composite drops below θ — system enters fragile regime
7. In fragile regime, Turchin secular cycle dynamics operate: elite overproduction, popular immiseration, eventually collapse
8. Collapse *restores* E and R — land becomes available, surveillance apparatus breaks down — resetting the cycle

## 5. Surplus Legibility and Information Infrastructure as Drivers

Two additional variables drive the lock-in sequence:

**L — Surplus legibility (0–1)**: Following Scott (2017), how measurable, countable, and extractable is the primary surplus? Foraging, swidden, and pastoral economies are inherently illegible (L ≈ 0.1–0.2). Cereal grain is uniquely legible: storable, measurable, transportable (L ≈ 0.6–0.8). The Inca case (L ≈ 0.9 with quipu administration but no writing) demonstrates that legibility is not the same as literacy — it is the *extractability* of surplus that matters.

**I — Information infrastructure (0–1)**: The existence and administrative deployment of systems for recording obligations, debts, ownership, and persons. Oral mnemonic systems (I ≈ 0.1–0.2), token/tally systems (I ≈ 0.3–0.4), partial writing (I ≈ 0.5–0.6), full literate bureaucracy (I ≈ 0.8–0.95). I is distinct from literacy per se: it measures the *administrative deployment* of information systems, not their cultural reach.

The Early Uruk / Late Uruk transition in the dataset provides the best-documented instance of the L→I→A→(suppress EDR) sequence. Early Uruk (c. 4000–3100 BCE) shows high L (grain-based agriculture in a river valley) and rising I (proto-cuneiform accounting), but no evidence of palace or hereditary kingship — S is moderate. Late Uruk / Early Dynastic (c. 3100–2700 BCE) shows the palace emerging as distinct from the temple, royal burials, and military expansion — S surges as I completes the administrative apparatus. EDR drops from ~0.48 to ~0.28 across this transition, passing through the estimated θ threshold.

## 6. Schismogenesis and Network Effects

One of G&W's most original contributions — and the component most absent from existing computational models — is the role of *schismogenesis* (Bateson 1935, applied by G&W) in governance formation. Societies do not simply respond to internal ecological and demographic conditions: they actively define themselves in contrast to their neighbours.

The Northwest Coast Potlatch societies developed their prestige-redistribution economics partly in conscious opposition to hierarchical chiefdom models visible among neighbouring groups. Zomia highland communities maintained illegible surplus and high mobility not through ecological constraint but through deliberate *state-avoidance* — keeping governance forms structurally resistant to incorporation. The Celtic tribal assemblies maintained deliberate non-urbanisation in the presence of Mediterranean urban models they could observe directly.

This has a structural implication: governance forms are not only responses to internal conditions (ecology, surplus, population density) but also to the governance landscape in which societies are embedded. The model requires:

- A network component: societies exist in relation to neighbouring governance systems
- A schismogenesis parameter: how strongly does a society differentiate from neighbours when adjacent configurations shift?
- A contagion parameter: how readily do governance innovations spread versus trigger counter-differentiation?

The `Comparators` column in the source dataset is an implicit edge list for this network component and provides a basis for initial schismogenesis modelling.

## 7. Integration with Collapse Models

Turchin's secular cycles (integrative and disintegrative phases driven by elite overproduction) are reframed in the isonomia model as follows:

The *integrative phase* corresponds to a SAP configuration where EDR remains above θ: popular pressure still constrains elite extraction through the self-correcting mechanism. The *disintegrative phase* begins when A has grown to the point where D is structurally suppressed — elites overshoot the extraction level that would previously have triggered resistance, because the feedback mechanism is broken.

Tainter's *declining marginal returns on complexity* is reframed as: the specific complexity that yields diminishing returns is administrative apparatus (A) once it has eliminated meaningful E and D. Forced inputs are always less efficient than voluntary ones; when the only available inputs are coerced, the overhead of maintaining coercion consumes increasing shares of surplus until the system cannot sustain itself.

Crucially, this reframing makes collapse a *regime transition rather than a failure*. Archaeological and historical evidence consistently shows that the populations of post-collapse "dark ages" often experienced improved nutrition, reduced working hours, and greater local autonomy — the standard of living for ordinary people improved even as population declined. The isonomia model predicts this: collapse restores E (land and mobility become available) and R (new institutional arrangements become possible), resetting the EDR composite above θ and restoring self-correcting capacity.

## 8. The Open Questions

Several questions remain unresolved and constitute a research agenda:

**The threshold value θ.** What EDR composite value marks the resilience threshold? Preliminary analysis suggests θ ≈ 0.4–0.5, but this requires cross-validation against known collapse events.

**Scale and egalitarianism.** G&W demonstrate that large-scale egalitarian societies existed. What are the scaling mechanisms — is it always ritual assembly, and is there an upper bound?

**Irreversibility.** Once below θ, are endogenous reform pathways possible? The Roman Gracchi reforms, various revolutionary transitions, and post-colonial constitution-building all suggest partial reversibility requiring specific conditions (elite fracture, external shock, or ideological reconfiguration).

**The measurement problem.** EDR variables are harder to operationalise from archaeological data than SAP variables. The coding scheme (see `data/coding_scheme.md`) establishes anchor cases and inter-rater reliability targets, but independent validation is needed.
