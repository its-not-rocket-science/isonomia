# Data Gap Analysis
## isonomia — Civilisational Emergence & Resilience Framework
### Based on Graeber-Wengrow theoretical requirements

---

## Summary of Existing Coverage

The `global-governance-models` dataset contains 41 entries with the following structural biases:

- **Region**: 12 of 41 entries are Greek political forms (29% of dataset); every other region has a single entry
- **Time**: 25 of 41 entries fall in 1000 BCE–500 CE; only 3 predate 1000 BCE; nothing post-1900
- **Type**: Governance types are highly granular but essentially one-per-type — no comparative variation within most categories
- **Framework gaps**: No entries for the key cases Graeber & Wengrow build their argument around

---

## Critical Gaps by Theoretical Function

### GAP 1 — Early Urbanism (the G&W pivot cases)
**Why critical:** G&W's most provocative claim is that the first cities were not hierarchical — that large-scale urban organisation precedes the state. The key cases are Çatalhöyük, early Uruk, Taosi, and Teotihuacan. Without these, the framework has no empirical anchor for its central claim about egalitarian urbanism at scale.

Missing entries needed:
| System | Approx. dates | Region | Why needed |
|---|---|---|---|
| Çatalhöyük | –7500 to –5700 | Anatolia | ~8,000 people, no evidence of hierarchy, no palace or temple complex |
| Early Uruk | –4000 to –3100 | Mesopotamia | Pre-state urbanism; the cuneiform/admin transition is the L/I lock-in moment |
| Late Uruk / Early Dynastic | –3100 to –2900 | Mesopotamia | The state emerges — contra to early Uruk; pair needed |
| Taosi | –2300 to –1800 | Northern China | Early Chinese urbanism; shows hierarchy formation and subsequent levelling |
| Teotihuacan | 100 to 550 CE | Mesoamerica | Largest pre-Columbian city; apartment compounds suggest radical equality |
| Mohenjo-daro / Indus Valley | –2600 to –1900 | South Asia | No palaces, no royal tombs, elaborate sanitation — the enigmatic case |
| Monte Albán | –500 to 700 | Mesoamerica | Zapotec urban centre, emergence from peer-polity competition |

### GAP 2 — Fertile Crescent Pre-State Forms
**Why critical:** The transition from flexible Natufian/PPNA communities to agricultural sedentism is the core of the "getting stuck" narrative. The dataset has nothing in this region before the Hittite Panku Council (–1650).

Missing entries needed:
| System | Approx. dates | Region | Why needed |

### GAP 8 — The SAP Framework Anchor Cases
**Why critical:** For the paper's argument to work, we need cases that clearly anchor each region of the SAP ternary space — including the extreme corners that existing entries don't occupy.

Currently underrepresented corners:
- **High-S / Low-A / Low-P**: Pure coercive monarchy without bureaucracy or competitive politics. Need: early Egyptian Old Kingdom, Shang Dynasty China
- **Low-S / High-A / Low-P**: Pure bureaucracy without sovereignty or competitive politics. Need: Inca administrative system (without treating the Sapa Inca as high-S)
- **Low-S / Low-A / High-P**: Pure prestige competition without hierarchy. Need: Highland New Guinea Big Man systems
- **High-S / High-A / High-P**: The "full lock-in" state. Need: Tang Dynasty China, Ottoman Empire at peak

Missing entries for SAP anchoring:
| System | Approx. dates | Region | SAP role |
|---|---|---|---|
| Egyptian Old Kingdom | –2686 to –2181 | Egypt | High-S anchor; pyramid construction as sovereignty display |
| Egyptian Middle Kingdom | –2055 to –1650 | Egypt | Transition case: S decreases, A increases |
| Shang Dynasty | –1600 to –1046 | China | High-S / oracle-bone divination as proto-A |
| Inca Empire (Tawantinsuyu) | 1438 to 1533 | Andean | High-A / quipu administration / no writing |
| Highland New Guinea Big Man | –1000 to 2000 | Papua New Guinea | High-P / pure competitive politics without S or A |
| Tang Dynasty | 618 to 907 | China | Full high-SAP state at scale |
| Ottoman Empire | 1299 to 1922 | Anatolia/Middle East | High-SAP with millet system (partial E preservation) |
| Mughal Empire | 1526 to 1857 | South Asia | High-SAP with religious pluralism dimension |

## Coding Scheme Notes

For the SAP and EDR indices, the following anchors should be applied consistently:

**Sovereignty (S):**
- 0.0 = No identifiable coercive authority (San Xaro Networks, New Guinea Big Man)
- 0.3 = Situational authority, can be refused (Gadaa System, Celtic Tribal Assembly)
- 0.6 = Established but constrained sovereignty (Roman Republic, Iroquois Confederacy)
- 0.9 = Near-absolute sovereign power (Zulu Monarchy, Egyptian Old Kingdom)

**Administration (A):**
- 0.0 = Oral tradition only, no records (San, Aboriginal, early Natufian)
- 0.3 = Tokens, tallies, mnemonic devices (early Ubaid, Inca quipu)
- 0.6 = Partial writing/record-keeping for specific purposes (early Uruk, Hittite)
- 0.9 = Full literate bureaucracy with census, taxation, legal code (Tang, Ottoman)

**Competitive Politics (P):**
- 0.0 = Deliberate suppression of competitive display (some millenarian movements)
- 0.3 = Personal prestige, non-transferable (most forager societies)
- 0.6 = Institutionalised competition for positions (Roman Republic, Iroquois)
- 0.9 = Politics as performance, display as governance (Balinese Negara, Theatrocracy)

**Exit Freedom (E):**
- 0.0 = Physically impossible to leave (debt bondage, serfdom at height)
- 0.3 = Possible but catastrophic cost (late Roman colonate, Tokugawa Japan)
- 0.6 = Possible with significant friction (most agricultural chiefdoms)
- 1.0 = Easy and normal (nomadic peoples, seasonal foragers)

**Disobedience Freedom (D):**
- 0.0 = Refusal = death (totalitarian states, extreme sovereignty)
- 0.3 = Refusal possible only through ritual/sanctioned channels
- 0.6 = Customary right to refuse with social cost
- 1.0 = Refusal expected and structurally built in (Gadaa age-set rotation)

**Arrangement Freedom (R):**
- 0.0 = Social structure entirely fixed by law or sacred order
- 0.3 = Minor variation permitted within rigid categories
- 0.6 = New institutions possible if they don't threaten existing hierarchy
- 1.0 = Deliberate seasonal or periodic reinvention of social arrangements
