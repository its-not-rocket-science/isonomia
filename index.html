The question

A book that asked the wrong question — in the best possible way

In 2021, the anthropologist David Graeber and archaeologist David Wengrow published The Dawn of Everything: A New History of Humanity. It was an immediate sensation. The argument was simple and explosive: for most of human history, people regularly and deliberately chose between radically different ways of organising themselves. Societies were not locked into fixed evolutionary stages — chiefdoms giving way to states giving way to democracies. They experimented. They argued. They walked away from hierarchies that didn't suit them and built new ones elsewhere. Freedom and political self-determination, Graeber and Wengrow suggested, were not modern inventions. They were ancient human capacities that many of our ancestors exercised quite consciously.

"The ultimate, hidden truth of the world is that it is something we make, and could just as easily make differently."

Graeber & Wengrow, The Dawn of Everything (2021)

The book was thrilling to read and genuinely important. But it raised an immediate empirical question: if societies really did have this capacity for self-determination — for disobedience, for political exit, for replacing their rulers — can we actually measure whether they used it? Can we compare Athens in 450 BCE with the Tang Dynasty in 700 CE, or the Tokugawa Shogunate with modern Norway, and say something rigorous about how free people actually were to push back against power?

Graeber and Wengrow worked primarily with deep historical and archaeological evidence — fascinating, but by its nature fragmentary and qualitative. What would happen if you took their core ideas seriously and tried to turn them into numbers?

That question is what the isonomia project set out to answer.

Why "isonomia"?

Isonomia (ἰσονομία) is an ancient Greek term meaning equality before the law — the equal right of citizens to participate in governance and to hold power accountable. The Athenians used it to describe the political conditions that made democracy possible. It is older than the word demokratia itself, and captures something more fundamental: not just that people vote, but that they have the practical freedom to refuse, resist, and replace those who govern them. That is the phenomenon this project measures.

The framework

Six numbers to describe a governance system

The first challenge was conceptual: what, exactly, do you measure? Political freedom is not a single thing. A society can have free elections but a secret police. It can permit vigorous public debate but make it physically impossible to leave. The Roman Republic held elections for centuries while progressively stripping those elections of any genuine power.

The isonomia framework addresses this by decomposing political freedom into six distinct dimensions, each coded independently on a scale from 0 to 1 for every governance system in the dataset.

S

Sovereignty index. How much concentrated coercive power does the ruling apparatus hold? High S = hard to resist.

A

Administrative capacity. How deeply does the state penetrate daily life — through census, taxation, education, surveillance?

P

Political competition. How genuinely contested is access to power? Can outsiders realistically challenge incumbents?

E

Exit freedom. Can people physically leave, relocate, or withdraw from the system without catastrophic cost?

D

Disobedience freedom. Can people refuse compliance, protest, or organise opposition without immediate severe punishment?

R

Replacement freedom. Can the governed actually remove and replace their rulers through legitimate means?

Of these six, D — disobedience freedom is the pivotal dimension. It is the closest modern analogue to Graeber and Wengrow's concept of societies that retained the capacity to say no. A society with high D has not just formal rights of protest but practical ones: the space to dissent without being disappeared, fired, impoverished, or killed.

Extending Graeber and Wengrow's model

Graeber and Wengrow were primarily interested in showing that alternatives existed — that early societies were not trapped in a linear evolutionary progression from bands to chiefdoms to states. The isonomia project takes their insight further in three ways.

First, it asks not just whether societies had political freedom, but how much — and whether that quantity can be tracked across time. Second, it asks what happens when freedom is lost: what triggers the suppression of D, and is it reversible? Third, it tests these questions against 2,600 years of evidence from 401 governance systems across every inhabited continent, validated against six independent modern political-science datasets.

The framework does not assume that freedom follows a single historical arc. It assumes precisely the opposite: that political freedom is unstable, contested, and can be won or lost at any point in history — exactly as Graeber and Wengrow argued.

How does the coding work?

Each governance system is assigned values for S, A, P, E, D, and R based on systematic reading of the historical and archaeological record. The coding draws on primary sources, existing political science datasets (including V-Dem, Polity5, the World Justice Project Rule of Law Index, and the Seshat global history database), and specialist historiography for each civilisation. The dataset is publicly available on GitHub; every coding decision is documented and auditable.

The resulting framework has been cross-validated against six external modern datasets. The correlations range from r = 0.60 (Seshat, using the oldest and most indirect evidence) to r = 0.92 (Freedom in the World). For a measure that spans Bronze Age city-states and contemporary welfare democracies, this is a remarkably strong set of validations.

The key insight

A map of political possibility

Plot any governance system's sovereignty (S) against its disobedience freedom (D) and something striking appears. Most systems don't scatter randomly across the space — they cluster. And they cluster in ways that reveal the fundamental tension at the heart of governance: the more power a ruling apparatus concentrates, the harder it becomes for citizens to say no.

But the relationship is not deterministic. Some systems sustain high D despite high S. Others lose their freedoms rapidly once they cross a critical threshold. The chart below, generated from the full 401-system dataset, shows the phase space — hover over any point to explore.

The horizontal dashed line at D = 0.45 is not arbitrary. Statistical analysis of the full dataset reveals it as a critical transition point: once a governance system's disobedience freedom falls below this threshold, the probability of recovery drops sharply. Systems above the line have a 6.46× higher odds ratio for avoiding full lock-in. The line separates the territory of political possibility from the territory of autocratic consolidation.

This is a quantitative version of something Graeber and Wengrow described qualitatively: there is a point of no return beyond which societies lose the practical capacity to change course.

The mechanism

How freedom is lost: the lock-in sequence

The single most important finding of the isonomia project is that the suppression of political freedom is not random. It follows a reproducible sequence — so reproducible that it appears across systems separated by thousands of years and tens of thousands of kilometres.

L → I → A → E↓ → D↓ → R↓

Reading this left to right: surplus resources (L) fund the construction of administrative apparatus (I → A). Once that apparatus is sufficiently developed, it begins to constrain the freedom to leave (E↓). As exit becomes harder, the freedom to disobey (D↓) follows, and finally the ability to replace rulers altogether (R↓) is foreclosed.

This is the lock-in sequence — and it maps remarkably well onto the history of state formation across the ancient world. The Mauryan Empire, the Tang Dynasty, the Roman Republic's transformation into the Principate, the Ottoman Empire under the Hamidian autocracy: all follow recognisable variants of this pattern.

D₀ as a critical moderator

Not all systems lock in equally. The key variable turns out to be the initial level of disobedience freedom — what the papers call D₀ — at the point when the lock-in process begins. Systems with high D₀ (starting above the θ = 0.45 threshold) show a 5× higher exit rate from the lock-in pathway. They have more room to organise, protest, and resist before the window closes.

This is a deeply important result. It means that political freedom is not just an outcome but a structural resource. Societies that begin with high D₀ are substantially harder to lock in. This is precisely why authoritarian consolidations so often target civil society, press freedom, and the right to organise first — not because these things are immediately threatening, but because they represent the structural D₀ that makes resistance possible later.

Societies that begin with high disobedience freedom are substantially harder to lock in. Authoritarian consolidations target civil society first because high D₀ is the structural resource that makes later resistance possible.

The appointment attractor

A related finding — discovered through an entirely separate model (a continuous-time Markov chain applied to succession mechanisms) — shows what happens to leadership replacement as lock-in progresses. As administrative capacity (A) rises and political competition (P) falls, governance systems converge on an appointment attractor: a stable state in which power is transferred by the incumbent to a chosen successor, rather than through any competitive or popular mechanism. The equilibrium probability of this appointment state reaches π = 0.595 in high-L systems — more than half of all potential stable states are appointment-based once resource surplus becomes large enough.

This is not a prediction about any particular ideology or period. It has appeared in Bronze Age palaces, mediaeval courts, twentieth-century single-party states, and contemporary hybrid regimes. The mechanism is structural, not cultural.

How suppression works

Not all lock-ins are the same

A key refinement of the basic lock-in model came from asking a more granular question: when D falls, how does it fall? Blunt coercion — secret police, imprisonment, physical violence — is one answer. But it is not the most durable one. The most enduring suppression of political freedom does not need continuous force. It works by making the idea of resistance culturally unavailable.

This is what the Italian Marxist Antonio Gramsci called hegemony: the ruling class does not merely compel compliance, it manufactures consent. Subjects internalise the worldview of those who govern them so thoroughly that alternatives become literally inconceivable. Michel Foucault described a related mechanism in his analysis of disciplinary societies: when populations are organised under sufficiently comprehensive surveillance, they police themselves. Obedience does not require a guard at every door because the awareness of potential observation is enough.

Four mechanisms, one outcome

The fifth paper in the isonomia series tested these ideas systematically across 30 governance systems for which detailed time-series data are available. It found four empirically distinguishable mechanisms by which D declines or recovers.

Hegemonic drift

D declines gradually as educational institutions, legal systems, and cultural norms normalise compliance. The Abbasid Caliphate is the paradigm case: freedom continued to erode for three centuries after the coercive capacity that established it had collapsed. The hegemonic apparatus had become self-reproducing.

Coercive maintenance

D is kept low through the direct application of state force. These systems typically begin with low D₀ and stay there. Unlike hegemonic systems, remove the coercive apparatus and the suppression may lift — but it is exhausting and expensive to maintain.

Counter-hegemony

D rises through the deliberate construction of civic institutions — trade unions, free press, independent courts — that embed a culture of dissent across generations. All three rising-D systems in the dataset (Britain, Switzerland, Norway) have sovereignty indices below 0.20: the state must be too weak to crush these institutions while they are forming.

Joint collapse

Both administrative capacity and disobedience freedom decline together under imperial overextension. The Ottoman Empire in its final century is the case study: as Tanzimat administrative modernisation failed and provincial control collapsed, the Hamidian autocracy intensified coercion to compensate — driving D down alongside A.

The Tokugawa anomaly

One case is particularly striking. The Tokugawa Shogunate in Japan (1603–1868) sits in the stable-low zone of the phase space: D remained suppressed throughout, with minimal change. But it was not maintained by active coercive deployment. Instead, it was produced by what Foucault would recognise as a panopticon — a system of mutual surveillance so comprehensive that disobedience was practically inconceivable. The mandatory residence system (sankin-kōtai), five-family mutual accountability groups (goningumi), temple registration, and hereditary status categories created a social architecture in which the state's presence did not need to be felt because it could always, in principle, be felt. The Shogunate inherited a population that had lived through 150 years of civil war and actively wanted order. It began at its equilibrium rather than drifting to it.

What we found

The headline results

Across six papers and a dataset spanning 2,600 years, a coherent picture has emerged. These are the findings that have survived statistical testing most robustly.

6.46×

Higher odds of avoiding lock-in for governance systems starting above the D = 0.45 threshold.

p = 0.014

Hegemonic systems begin with significantly higher D than coercive systems — confirming the distinction is real and measurable.

r = −0.326

Pooled correlation between administrative capacity expansion and disobedience freedom decline across 79 historical intervals in 9 systems.

π = 0.595

Equilibrium probability of appointment-based succession in high-surplus governance systems — the "appointment attractor."

S ≤ 0.20

Sovereignty threshold below which counter-hegemonic D recovery becomes possible. Above it, the state can always crush dissent while it is forming.

3 centuries

Duration for which Abbasid hegemonic suppression of D persisted after the coercive capacity that established it had fully collapsed.

What this means for today

The isonomia findings are historical, but their implications are not limited to the past. The lock-in sequence has been observed in enough modern governance systems — including some that many observers would currently classify as democratic — to suggest that the structural mechanisms identified here are active in the present.

The D₀ result is particularly relevant. The evidence suggests that the most effective protection against autocratic consolidation is not the response to it — it is the prior existence of a high-D political culture, with the civic institutions, legal traditions, and cultures of organised dissent that sustain it. Dismantling those institutions, even incrementally, reduces the structural resource available for resistance later.

Similarly, the hegemonic inertia finding carries a sober implication: removing coercive apparatus may be insufficient to restore freedom in societies where the normalisation of compliance has become deeply embedded. The Abbasid case shows that even after the coercive apparatus is gone, D may continue to fall for centuries — because the educational, juridical, and cultural infrastructure of compliance has become self-reproducing.

The most striking feature of Panel A is what it shows about the three rising-D systems — Britain, Switzerland, and Norway — in the top-right of the post-1600 cluster. They are not just exceptions to the general downward trend. They are cases where the counter-hegemonic pathway worked: where civic institutions were built while sovereign capacity was still low enough not to crush them, and where those institutions became durable enough to sustain rising D over centuries.

Panel D tells the Soviet story with particular clarity. The plunge to near-zero D during the Stalin terror (1937), the partial recovery under de-Stalinisation, the second suppression under Brezhnev, and the rapid expansion during glasnost and demokratizatsiya — all in 74 years, all measurable and comparable with the Roman Republic's 473-year decline or the Abbasid Caliphate's 550-year trajectory.

The journey

How the project developed

  • Starting point

    Graeber & Wengrow's challenge

    The Dawn of Everything proposes that human societies have always had the capacity for radical political self-determination. The isonomia project asks: can this be formalised and tested?

  • Foundation

    The SAP-EDR coding framework

    401 governance systems coded across six dimensions (S, A, P, E, D, R). Cross-validated against V-Dem, Polity5, WJP, Freedom in the World, CCP, and Seshat. Correlations range from r = 0.60 to r = 0.92.

  • Core finding

    The lock-in sequence discovered

    The L→I→A→E↓→D↓→R↓ sequence documented. D₀ identified as the pivotal moderator. The θ = 0.45 critical threshold established. The appointment attractor modelled via CTMC.

  • Deepening

    Schismogenesis and network dynamics

    Bateson's schismogenesis applied to governance networks via agent-based modelling. The degree-4 sign reversal reproduced. Three Graeber-Wengrow case studies confirmed computationally.

  • Refinement

    Disaggregating D-suppression

    The mechanism question: not just whether D falls, but how. Four pathways identified. The Gramsci-Foucault-Bateson synthesis operationalised. The r(ΔA,ΔD) sign classifier proposed.

  • Next steps

    Hegemonic drift analysis (Paper 5)

    Thirty governance systems, 2,600 years of D-trajectory data, nine systems with matched A-trajectories. Submitted to Social Evolution & History.

Open research

The data and code are open

All data, analysis scripts, and coding documentation are publicly available in the isonomia GitHub repository. The project is committed to full reproducibility: every statistical result reported in the papers can be replicated from the source data using the provided scripts.

The dataset includes:

  • governance_extended.csv — the full 401-system dataset with all six primary dimensions plus extended trajectory fields
  • d_trajectory_parsed.csv — time-series D codings for 30 systems with trajectory data
  • hegemony_index.csv — computed hegemony indices and trajectory classifications for the 30-system subsample
  • hegemonic_drift.py — the analysis script for Paper 5, producing all reported statistical results

The repository also contains draft manuscripts for Papers 2–5 and the submitted versions of Papers 1 and the Cliodynamics synthesis.
